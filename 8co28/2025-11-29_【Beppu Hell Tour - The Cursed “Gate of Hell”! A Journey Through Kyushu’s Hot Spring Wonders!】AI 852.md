【Beppu Hell Tour（Jigoku Meguri）: The Cursed “Gate of Hell”! A Journey Through Kyushu’s Hot Spring Wonders!】AI852 SeeTheWorld - Travel

Beppu is located in Oita Prefecture, Kyushu, Japan, and its world‑famous as the “Hot Spring Kingdom.” There are over two thousand hot spring sources here, with a daily output of 130,000 tons of hot water. Both the amount of water and the number of sources rank number one in Japan. Beppu’s hot spring history goes back more than 1,300 years, shaped by volcanic activity from Mount Tsurumi, which created all kinds of different spring qualities. These geothermal wonders don’t just attract people to soak and heal; they’ve also given rise to unique attractions. 
The Cursed Land 
The Beppu Hell Tour (別府地獄めぐり) is Beppu’s most iconic sightseeing route, featuring seven “hell” hot springs with water temperatures soaring above 98°C. They’re for viewing only, not for bathing. These hells got their names from violent boiling, steam eruptions, bizarre colors, and strong sulfur smells. Back in the day, they were even seen as cursed places. Legends record real “curse” incidents: for example, a hunter chasing a deer slipped and fell into the Blood Pond Hell, swallowed by boiling mud, and people said he was “dragged into hell.” A farmer inhaled high‑concentration hydrogen sulfide, fainted, and woke up insane, so villagers believed his “soul was taken.” When Mount Tsurumi erupted, villagers thought “the Gate of Hell had opened” and held exorcism rituals to close it. These tragedies spread by word of mouth, turning into the taboo that “anyone who gets close to hell will suffer retribution.” Later, Western geology came to Japan, and scholars studied volcanic geothermal activity, proving that “hell” was just a natural phenomenon. Today, the “hells” have become national scenic spots, showing off the incredible power of nature. 
The Seven Hot Spring Hells
 • Blood Pond Hell (Chinoike Jigoku): The oldest natural hell in Japan, with over 1,300 years of history. The pond water, rich in iron oxide and magnesium, turns bright red. Boiling mud bubbles up like flames of hell. It’s 30 meters deep, with water at 78°C. From the observation deck you can see the whole view. There’s a foot bath area, and the local specialty “Blood Pond Ointment” is said to help skin problems.
 • Tatsumaki Hell: A rare geyser that erupts every 30 to 40 minutes. The hot water column shoots up 30 meters high at 105°C, roaring like a tornado. Visitors can watch safely behind fences, and before the eruption you can check out nearby Blood Pond Hell to save time.
 • Umi Hell: Famous for its dreamy cobalt‑blue water, formed by iron sulfate springs. It’s 200 meters deep, with water at 98°C. Steam drifts all around, making it look more like a blue paradise than a hell. The grounds include a Japanese garden, with scenery that changes by season. You can also try hot spring eggs or “hell‑steamed” pudding.
 • Oniishibozu Hell: Gray mud bubbles up, looking like bald monks’ heads.
 • Kamado Hell: Offers different hot spring experiences, including drinking spring water and steam cooking.
 • Oniyama Hell: Home to over a hundred crocodiles, with feeding shows.
 • Shiraike Hell: Milky‑white pond water paired with tropical fish tanks, calm and elegant.
Travel Tips 
Visiting all seven hell hot springs takes about two to three hours. Make sure to wear comfortable shoes. Pair the trip with local hell‑steamed food, and enjoy the full feast of the “Hot Spring Kingdom”!
